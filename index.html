What is Nabemono?

Nabemono, often referred to as "nabe," is a popular Japanese stew that's particularly enjoyed during the colder months. The term "nabe" literally means "pot," reflecting the communal style in which these dishes are often cooked and served right at the dining table. Nabemono is a broad category that includes a variety of hot pot dishes, characterized by their preparation in a donabe (a traditional Japanese earthenware pot) or a deep skillet. Ingredients can vary widely but typically include a mix of meats (like chicken, pork, beef, or seafood), vegetables (such as cabbage, mushrooms, and green onions), tofu, and sometimes noodles or dumplings, all simmered together in a flavorful broth. There are several popular types of nabemono, each with its own distinct ingredients and broth flavors:

Sukiyaki

A sweet and savory hot pot with thinly sliced beef, vegetables, tofu, and noodles, cooked in a soy sauce-based broth sweetened with sugar and mirin.

Shabu-Shabu

Thinly sliced meat and vegetables cooked in a lighter broth. Diners cook the ingredients themselves at the table by swishing them back and forth in the hot broth, hence the name "shabu-shabu" (mimicking the sound of this action). It's served with dipping sauces, like ponzu or sesame sauce.

Kimchi nabe

A spicy hot pot influenced by Korean cuisine, featuring kimchi, vegetables, meats, and often tofu, cooked in a spicy and flavorful broth.
