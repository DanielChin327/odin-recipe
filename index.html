What is Nabemono?

Nabemono, often referred to as "nabe," is a popular Japanese stew that's particularly enjoyed during the colder months. The term "nabe" literally means "pot," reflecting the communal style in which these dishes are often cooked and served right at the dining table. Nabemono is a broad category that includes a variety of hot pot dishes, characterized by their preparation in a donabe (a traditional Japanese earthenware pot) or a deep skillet. Ingredients can vary widely but typically include a mix of meats (like chicken, pork, beef, or seafood), vegetables (such as cabbage, mushrooms, and green onions), tofu, and sometimes noodles or dumplings, all simmered together in a flavorful broth. There are several popular types of nabemono, each with its own distinct ingredients and broth flavors. This one is Mizutaki. A chicken based one.

Ingredients

  • 400g chicken (thigh or breast), cut into bite-sized pieces
  • 1/2 napa cabbage, chopped
  • 2 carrots, sliced
  • 200g shiitake mushrooms
  • 1 block of tofu, cut into cubes
  • 2 scallions, chopped
  • 1 liter chicken or vegetable broth
  • Soy sauce, to taste
  • Salt and pepper, to taste
  • Ponzu sauce (for dipping)
  • Have some cooked rice on the side or have some udon noodles

Recipe

  1. Prepare the ingredients: Clean and chop the vegetables, slice the chicken, and cut the tofu into cubes.
  2. In a large pot, bring the broth to a boil. Season it with a little soy sauce, salt, and pepper according to your taste.
  3. First, add the chicken pieces to the boiling broth and let them cook for about 5 minutes, or until they start to turn white.
  4. Add the harder vegetables first, such as carrots, and let them simmer for a few minutes.
  5. Follow with the rest of the vegetables and tofu. Let the hotpot simmer until all ingredients are cooked through, about 10-15 minutes. Make sure to not overcook the vegetables.
  6. Adjust the seasoning of the broth as necessary. You can add more soy sauce, salt, or pepper according to your preference.
  7. Serve hot in bowls, and enjoy the Mizutaki with ponzu sauce and optional chili oil for dipping the cooked ingredients.
